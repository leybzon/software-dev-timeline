Day before yesterday · Overview

Mainframes dominated. Code was written on paper, transcribed to punch cards, handed to operators, run overnight. One typo cost a day. Hardware was scarce; programmer time was cheap — the inverse of today.

Why include this era: it reminds the audience that every era looks normal until it's replaced. Today's IDEs will look like punch cards in 20 years.

ERA 00 / 03 · LENS 01 SKILLS02 / 20N for notes

DBY · Skills & Developer Experience

The DBY programmer was closer to a mathematician than an engineer. Specialization was extreme — you mastered one language (COBOL, Fortran, PL/I) and stayed in it for a career. Hardware awareness was non-negotiable: you optimized for memory and CPU cycles because both were scarce.

Developer experience was slow and solitary. Feedback loops measured in hours or days. Debugging meant reading hex dumps and printouts. Pair programming didn't exist. The job rewarded patience and precision over speed.

ERA 00 / 03 · LENS 02 AGILITY03 / 20N for notes

DBY · Agility & Cadence

"Agility" wasn't a concept yet — there was nothing to be agile against. Big mainframe programs shipped in annual or multi-year cycles. Maintenance releases trickled out as patch tapes mailed to customers.

The feedback loop dominated the engineer's day: a single compile-and-run cycle took 24 hours. Whole careers were built around minimizing the number of times you needed to "try it." This is the unstated baseline that Agile (2001) was a reaction to.

ERA 00 / 03 · LENS 03 COST04 / 20N for notes

DBY · Cost & Economics

The economics were the inverse of today. Hardware dominated — a System/360 in 1965 rented for ~$8K/month (~$80K today), and most organizations rented because outright purchase was beyond reach. Software was bundled with hardware at no separate charge until IBM's 1969 unbundling decision, which effectively created the independent software industry.

People were a relatively small line item. Programmers were well-paid but few; the bulk of the budget went to iron and the room to put it in (raised floors, climate control, dedicated electrical service).

ERA 00 / 03 · LENS 04 MACRO05 / 20N for notes

DBY · Macro Context

The era was shaped by government spending and one dominant vendor. NASA, the Department of Defense, and similar institutions were the largest customers, and the budgets they signed defined the shape of the industry. IBM held over 70% market share for most of the period.

The 1969 unbundling — forced by an antitrust suit — was the single most important economic event of the era. It separated software pricing from hardware pricing and created the independent software industry that would explode in the next era.

Yesterday · Skills & Developer Experience

The senior engineer of this era was a deep stack specialist. Java EE or .NET, sometimes LAMP. You knew the framework's quirks intimately, you'd argue about design patterns, and you'd been doing it for a decade. Career progression was vertical within one platform — Junior → Senior → Architect.

Developer experience: build, package, hand off, wait. Bugs found in QA bounced back as tickets. Production was someone else's problem. The phrase "it works on my machine" became a meme because it was true.

ERA 01 / 03 · LENS 02 AGILITY08 / 20N for notes

Yesterday · Agility & Cadence

The Agile Manifesto was signed in 2001, but adoption was uneven and political. Most enterprises stayed on waterfall well into the late 2000s. Even teams that "did agile" often ran quarterly major releases with the sprints being internal scaffolding rather than ship cadence.

"Release manager" was a full-time role. Production deployments happened on Friday nights or Saturday mornings, with a war room, runbooks, and rollback plans. Continuous integration tools (CruiseControl, Hudson, Jenkins) were beginning to appear but were not yet default.

ERA 01 / 03 · LENS 03 COST09 / 20N for notes

Yesterday · Cost & Economics

Costs were fragmented across many heavy line items. Datacenters consumed CAPEX. Enterprise licenses (Oracle, WebSphere, Windows Server) consumed OPEX at per-CPU pricing that made architecture decisions financial decisions. In-house engineering was expensive in the US and Western Europe, which drove the offshoring boom — India and Eastern Europe became the first big global arbitrage on developer labor.

The economic model: everything cost something specific. Want to add capacity? Buy more boxes. Want more features? Hire more people. The marginal cost of software was the cost of the things underneath it.

ERA 01 / 03 · LENS 04 MACRO10 / 20N for notes

Yesterday · Macro Context

The era was bookended by two market crashes — the dot-com bust (2000) and the global financial crisis (2008). Both reshaped IT spending and pushed companies toward cost discipline, which in turn accelerated offshoring and open source adoption (free as in "doesn't need a PO").

The two most consequential events for our story were quiet at the time: AWS launching in 2006 and the iPhone in 2007. Both planted the seeds of the next era — cloud as the new platform, mobile as the new client. By 2010 the shape of "today" was visible if you squinted.

ERA 02 / 03 · LENS 01 SKILLS12 / 20N for notes

Today · Skills & Developer Experience

The senior engineer of today is broader than ever before. Full-stack literacy is table stakes. DevOps mindset — you own your service in production — is expected. Product sense and communication matter as much as raw coding ability.

The newest layer: AI fluency. Knowing how to scope a task for an agent, write a good prompt, recognize when the output is wrong, and integrate AI tools into your day without losing your judgment. The most productive engineers right now aren't the fastest typists — they're the best at decomposing problems and reviewing AI-generated work.

Tension to name on stage: the same tools that make seniors more productive may be eroding the apprenticeship that produces the next generation of seniors.

ERA 02 / 03 · LENS 02 AGILITY13 / 20N for notes

Today · Agility & Cadence

For mature teams, "release" no longer means what it meant. Deploys happen many times a day; feature flags decouple "deployed" from "released". The unit of risk has shrunk from "the quarterly release" to "this one PR behind a flag."

DORA metrics (deployment frequency, lead time, change failure rate, time to restore) became the lingua franca for measuring engineering health. The 2024 DORA report shows AI tools correlated with higher individual productivity but mixed effects on team-level throughput — an honest tension worth naming.

ERA 02 / 03 · LENS 03 COST14 / 20N for notes

Today · Cost & Economics

Cloud collapsed the cost of starting. A two-person team with $100K and six months can ship a product that would have required a 50-person team and $5M two decades ago. But cloud bills became a new fixed cost — large enough to spawn an entire discipline (FinOps) dedicated to managing them.

Engineers became the largest line item by far. Smaller, more senior, more expensive teams replaced large mid-level rosters. AI inference is the newest line item — small today (3–5% for most companies), growing fast, and unevenly distributed (AI-native products vs. traditional SaaS).

ERA 02 / 03 · LENS 04 MACRO15 / 20N for notes

Today · Macro Context

The era split into two regimes. 2010–2021 (ZIRP): near-zero interest rates, cheap capital, hyper-growth as the operating model, headcount as the metric. 2022–today (efficiency reckoning): rate hikes, layoffs across the industry, smaller teams, AI productivity gains used to rationalize restructuring.

Late 2022 stacked two shocks in the same quarter: ChatGPT's public release and the start of the tech layoff cycle. The two narratives — "AI makes engineers more productive" and "we need fewer engineers" — collided immediately and haven't fully resolved.

Geopolitics entered software: chip export controls, CHIPS Act, sovereignty concerns about cloud. Compute is no longer just a cost — it's a strategic and political asset.

ERA 03 / 03 · LENS 01 SKILLS17 / 20N for notes

Tomorrow · Skills & Developer Experience

The engineer's work moves up the stack of abstraction. Writing code becomes the cheapest part. Writing the spec — the precise statement of what should happen — and the evals — the precise statement of how we'll know — becomes the expensive and valuable part.

Taste and judgment become explicitly named skills. "Which of these 12 candidate implementations should we ship?" is a question with real consequences and no obvious answer. The senior engineer's value is in knowing which trade-offs matter.

The hard tension: if AI does the entry-level coding, where does the next generation learn the craft? This is the unanswered question of the era. Companies that solve it (apprenticeships, deliberate scaffolding, code archaeology assignments) may end up with a structural advantage.

ERA 03 / 03 · LENS 03 COST19 / 20N for notes

Tomorrow · Cost & Economics

The marginal cost of writing software trends toward the cost of model calls. MVP cost collapses to a few thousand dollars. The supply of software expands dramatically, which shifts competitive differentiation from "who can build it" to distribution, brand, taste, and judgment.

For larger products, compute becomes a real line item — enterprise AI bills measured in millions per year. FinOps for AI emerges as its own discipline. Engineering orgs default to smaller and more senior. The total dollar spend on engineering may go down even as the output goes up — a politically charged shift inside companies.

ERA 03 / 03 · LENS 04 MACRO20 / 20N for notes

Tomorrow · Macro Context

The forces shaping tomorrow are no longer software-internal. Capex on AI infrastructure is reshaping the cloud landscape — hyperscalers and frontier labs are building datacenters at a scale not seen since the original telecom buildout. Energy emerges as the binding constraint: chips can be made, but powering them at scale runs into grid limits.

Geopolitics deepens: chip export controls, compute sovereignty as a national policy concern, debates over open-weight vs. closed-weight models as questions of industrial strategy.

Inside companies, smaller engineering orgs become the new default. The IT services industry — body-shopping, offshore development, large consulting practices — faces structural pressure as the work they sell becomes automatable. Adaptation is uneven; the transition is unlikely to be quiet.

Closing line for the talk: "Yesterday we hired hands. Today we hire minds. Tomorrow we hire judgment."
